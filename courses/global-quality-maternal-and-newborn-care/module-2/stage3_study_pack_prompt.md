## 1. One-paragraph summary

This transdisciplinary US-based study developed and applied the Midwifery Integration Scoring System (MISS), a weighted composite tool built from 50 of 110 regulatory indicators, to quantify the degree to which midwives are integrated into state-level maternity care systems across all 50 US states and the District of Columbia. The MISS scoring system captures differences in scope of practice, provider autonomy, governance, prescriptive authority, interprofessional collaboration, and access to care across birth settings, drawing on a database of published regulatory data validated by 92 state and national regulatory experts. Using 2014 CDC Vital Statistics data, the study calculated correlation coefficients between MISS scores and maternal-newborn outcomes including rates of spontaneous vaginal delivery, cesarean section, vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC), breastfeeding, preterm birth, low birth weight, and neonatal mortality. Higher MISS scores correlated significantly with better outcomes across all these measures. The study also found that states with lower MISS scores had significantly lower density of midwives and reduced access to care across birth settings, and that Black, Hispanic, and white babies all experienced lower neonatal mortality in states with higher integration scores, with midwifery integration explaining variance in neonatal mortality outcomes above and beyond the proportion of African American births per state.

## 2. Key maternal/newborn care points

- MISS scores ranged from 17 (North Carolina, lowest) to 61 (Washington State, highest) out of a possible 100 points, indicating that even the most integrated US states fall below two-thirds of the conditions required for a fully integrated midwifery system.
- Higher MISS scores were significantly associated with higher rates of spontaneous vaginal delivery, VBAC, and breastfeeding at birth and at six months, and significantly lower rates of cesarean section, preterm birth, low birth weight infants, and neonatal death.
- Between 2004 and 2014, community birth rates increased by an average of 72% across US states; states with higher MISS scores had significantly higher community birth rates in 2014 and significantly larger increases in community births over that period.
- The proportion of Non-Hispanic Black births per state accounted for 38.5% of differences in neonatal mortality scores; MISS scores explained an additional 11.6% of variance, and together these two factors predicted 50.1% of the variance in neonatal mortality rates across states.
- MISS scores were significantly lower in states with a higher proportion of Non-Hispanic Black births, and density of and access to midwives were also significantly lower in those states, indicating a compounding equity burden for Black childbearing populations.
- Licensure of Certified Professional Midwives (CPMs) alone correlated with access to midwifery care in community settings but was not synonymous with integration and did not confer the same outcome benefits as full integration scores.
- Approximately 10% of US births are attended by midwives, compared to 50–75% in other high-resource OECD countries; in 2015, 89.8% of US births were attended by physicians, 8.5% by CNMs/CMs, and 0.8% by other midwives including CPMs.
- Poor communication, disagreement, and lack of clarity around provider roles are identified in the literature cited by this article as primary determinants of intrapartum neonatal and maternal deaths during critical obstetric events.
- A cited study found that in states where CNMs have greater professional autonomy, without a requirement for physician supervision, rates of surgical birth, preterm birth, and low birth weight were lower even after adjusting for maternal age, parity, race, education, marital status, cigarette use, and prenatal care utilization.
- The study notes that the US precedent of restricting qualified attendants across birth settings and prioritising institutional birth has been influential in low- and middle-income countries, and that incentivising institutional birth and physician management of healthy pregnancies has exacerbated gaps between demand and available health human resources globally.

## 1. One-paragraph summary

This peer-reviewed commentary, authored by fourteen midwifery, public health, and global health leaders from institutions including the University of Dundee, WHO, UNFPA, UNICEF, Yale, and JHPIEGO, argues that midwifery is a demonstrated, evidence-based solution to global maternal and newborn mortality and morbidity, and asks why systemic barriers continue to block global progress in implementing it at scale. The authors identify the core problem as the intersectionality of gender, social, professional, and economic disempowerment: midwives and the women they serve are both predominantly female and both subject to the same structural discrimination, leading to chronic under-investment, inadequate remuneration, conflation of midwifery with nursing, exclusion from leadership, and fragmented care with consequent safety and quality gaps. The commentary documents the transformative potential of quality midwifery — including reductions in maternal and newborn mortality, stillbirth, and preterm birth; prevention of unnecessary obstetric interventions; and improved access in rural and remote communities — drawing on evidence from the 2014 Lancet Midwifery Series and the Cochrane review of midwife-led continuity models. It concludes with a call for global action grounded in gender equality, midwifery-led academic leadership, partnership with women and women's advocacy groups, and implementation of International Confederation of Midwives (ICM) international standards, including through the 2019 WHO framework for strengthening midwifery education for Universal Health Coverage 2030.

## 2. Key maternal/newborn care points

- Growing evidence across multiple methods shows that quality midwifery care — defined as knowledgeable, skilled, and compassionate care across the continuum from pregnancy through birth and beyond — saves lives, reduces preterm birth, promotes health and well-being, and improves sustainability of health systems.
- Widespread misunderstanding of midwifery's role and scope persists at all levels of policy, health services, academia, and among funders, resulting in fragmented care with inevitable safety and quality gaps, and contributing to the unprecedented rise in unnecessary and unsafe obstetric interventions.
- Research investment has been systematically skewed toward obstetric clinical and emergency interventions, with far less research focused on enhancing respectful, supportive, women- and newborn-centred quality care for all; community-based studies in low-income countries have conducted over 100 trials on non-professional health workers while professional midwives have been studied far less.
- Systemic barriers to midwifery are rooted in the intersectionality of gender inequality, professional bias, and economic disempowerment: many midwives are inadequately remunerated, overwhelmed by workload, exposed to sexual and other forms of violence, and excluded from senior leadership positions.
- Countries with long-established midwifery — including the Nordic countries — consistently demonstrate very low rates of maternal and newborn mortality; countries that have strengthened midwifery as part of the health system have seen maternal mortality fall and quality of care improve.
- High-quality midwifery — enabled by quality education, professional regulation, an enabling health system, and multidisciplinary team working — contributes to reducing maternal and newborn mortality and stillbirth; preventing escalating unnecessary interventions; reducing disrespect and abuse in childbirth; increasing access in remote and rural areas; improving early childhood development; and strengthening health system sustainability.
- Countries that have successfully strengthened midwifery in recent years (cited as Canada, New Zealand, Australia, the UK, and Malawi) have done so by simultaneously strengthening midwifery-led academic leadership and forming partnerships with women and women's advocacy groups; interdisciplinary support has also been key.
- Countries in sub-Saharan Africa (Ghana, Zambia, Somalia) and South Asia (Bangladesh, Nepal, India) are making progress in strengthening midwifery and implementing international standards as of the time of publication (2019).
- The 2019 WHO Global Strategy for Women's, Children's and Adolescent Health includes a seven-step action plan for countries working towards international-standard midwifery education.
- Midwives enabled to practise to their full scope within enabling health systems and multidisciplinary teams act as powerful human rights defenders for women and children; tackling systemic barriers rooted in gender inequality is described as fundamental to achieving universal health coverage and the SDGs.

# Final Synthesis: Global Quality Maternal and Newborn Care

## 1. The Global Imperative for Quality Maternal and Newborn Care

Despite increased access to healthcare facilities globally, significant numbers of **newborns die (2.7 million annually)**, **women die from pregnancy/childbirth complications (300,000 annually)**, and **babies are stillborn (2.6 million annually)** [Source Card 12]. This persistent challenge highlights a critical gap between knowing what effective interventions exist and actually implementing them on a global scale [Source Card 12]. Poor quality of care is identified as a **major barrier** to reducing maternal and newborn mortality and morbidity, particularly in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) [Source Card 09, Source Card 12]. Improving the quality of maternal and newborn care is therefore essential for achieving global health goals, including the Sustainable Development Goals [Source Card 12].

## 2. Defining and Measuring Quality: Beyond Clinical Outcomes

Quality of care extends beyond mere survival and clinical coverage; it encompasses the entire experience of care, including how women are treated by providers [Source Card 01]. The World Health Organization (WHO) provides a comprehensive framework for improving quality, outlining eight global standards that cover:
*   **Provision of evidence-based care** [Source Card 08].
*   **Effective communication** [Source Card 08].
*   **Respectful and preserved dignity** [Source Card 08].
*   **Emotional support** [Source Card 08].
*   **Competent and motivated human resources** [Source Card 08].
*   **Availability of essential physical resources** [Source Card 08].
*   **Information systems** for monitoring and improvement [Source Card 08].
*   **Effective referral systems** [Source Card 08].

### 2.1 Respectful Maternity Care (RMC) and Human Rights

Respectful Maternity Care (RMC) is recognized as a **fundamental human right** for childbearing women [Source Card 06]. The RMC Charter, developed by the White Ribbon Alliance, articulates seven universal rights:
*   Freedom from harm and ill-treatment [Source Card 06].
*   The right to information, informed consent, and refusal [Source Card 06].
*   The right to privacy and confidentiality [Source Card 06].
*   The right to dignity and respect [Source Card 06].
*   The right to equality, freedom from discrimination, and equitable care [Source Card 06].
*   The right to timely healthcare and to the highest attainable standard of health [Source Card 06].
*   The right to liberty, autonomy, and freedom from coercion [Source Card 06].

Evidence indicates that disrespect and abuse during childbirth are widespread and undermine women’s human rights [Source Card 06].

### 2.2 Culturally Appropriate Care

Providing **culturally appropriate skilled maternity care** is strongly recommended by WHO, based on moderate quality evidence [Source Card 11]. This type of care respects women's beliefs, values, and choices, and is associated with increased access to care, improved satisfaction, and better health outcomes for mothers and newborns [Source Card 11]. Health care providers are encouraged to develop cultural competencies through training, and health systems must establish supportive policies and environments to facilitate such care [Source Card 11].

## 3. The Reality of Mistreatment and Obstetric Violence

Empirical evidence reveals that mistreatment during facility-based childbirth is a prevalent issue. A WHO-led cross-sectional study across four LMICs (Ghana, Guinea, Myanmar, and Nigeria) found that **more than one-third of women experienced mistreatment**, with **41.6%** of observed women experiencing at least one form of physical or verbal abuse, stigma, or discrimination [Source Card 01].

### 3.1 Forms of Mistreatment and Abuse
Common forms of mistreatment include:
*   **Physical abuse:** Most commonly slapping, hitting, or punching, identified in approximately **14%** of observed women [Source Card 01].
*   **Verbal abuse:** Shouting, scolding, and mocking, recorded in **38%** of women [Source Card 01].
*   **Non-consented clinical procedures:** This was highly prevalent, with **75% of episiotomies**, **59% of vaginal examinations**, and **13% of caesarean births** performed without consent [Source Card 01].
*   **Stigma and discrimination** [Source Card 01].
*   **Poor rapport or failure to meet professional standards of care** [Source Card 01].

Women are at the highest risk of physical and verbal abuse during the critical period **30 minutes before birth to 15 minutes after birth** [Source Card 01]. Younger and less-educated women are disproportionately at risk, highlighting how existing social inequalities are replicated within health facilities [Source Card 01].

### 3.2 Obstetric Violence
The concept of **obstetric violence** further defines this mistreatment as a form of gender-based violence experienced during childbirth within institutional medical settings [Source Card 07]. It encompasses physical, psychological, and emotional harm, often manifesting as a lack of informed consent, coercive medical procedures, disrespectful treatment, and the denial of a birthing person's autonomy and human rights [Source Card 07]. This violence can contribute to long-term trauma for the birthing person [Source Card 07].

## 4. Systemic Barriers to Quality Care Delivery

Several systemic factors impede the delivery of high-quality maternal and newborn care, particularly in LMICs:

### 4.1 Resource and Infrastructure Deficits
A study in Kenya highlighted that midwifery performance, crucial for maternal and child health, is significantly hampered by:
*   **Lack of essential resources:** Basic delivery kits, partographs, and life-saving drugs such as oxytocin and magnesium sulfate [Source Card 03].
*   **Poor infrastructure** [Source Card 03].

### 4.2 Human Resources and Workforce Challenges
Midwives' performance is also negatively affected by:
*   **Inadequate staffing** and heavy workloads [Source Card 03].
*   **Long working hours** [Source Card 03].
*   **Low morale** due to poor remuneration and limited career progression [Source Card 03].
*   **Insufficient access to in-service training** and continuous professional development (CPD) [Source Card 03].

### 4.3 Gaps in Clinical Adherence
A systematic review and meta-analysis of care in LMICs identified significant gaps in adherence to basic, evidence-based clinical guidelines for common maternal and newborn complications:
*   Only **44.5%** of women received uterotonics for postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) [Source Card 09].
*   Only **50.1%** of women received magnesium sulfate for pre-eclampsia/eclampsia [Source Card 09].
*   Adherence to active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) was **63.8%** [Source Card 09].
*   Only **55.4%** of newborns received immediate umbilical cord care [Source Card 09].

### 4.4 Appropriate Use of Interventions: Caesarean Sections
While caesarean sections (CS) are crucial for saving lives when medically indicated, WHO states that rates above **10-15%** are not associated with further reductions in maternal or newborn mortality [Source Card 05]. The focus should be on ensuring access to medically necessary CS while avoiding unnecessary procedures, emphasizing the importance of data collection and quality of care for all CS [Source Card 05].

## 5. Strategies for Quality Improvement

Improving the quality of maternal and newborn care requires multifaceted interventions at facility, community, and system levels.

### 5.1 Quality Improvement (QI) Strategies
A systematic review of QI strategies in maternal and newborn healthcare settings, primarily in LMICs, found that most successful initiatives involve **multiple components** [Source Card 04]. Effective QI can improve health worker performance, adherence to clinical protocols, and service utilization [Source Card 04].

Common QI strategies include:
*   **Facility-based interventions:** Audit and feedback mechanisms, clinical training, supportive supervision, and the use of job aids and clinical guidelines [Source Card 04]. Supportive supervision and continuous professional development (CPD) are crucial for enhancing midwifery performance [Source Card 03].
*   **Community-based approaches:** Women's groups and community health worker (CHW) involvement can improve demand for and access to maternal and newborn health services [Source Card 04].

### 5.2 Community-Based Maternal and Newborn Care (CMNC)
CMNC interventions, such as CHW home visits and peer support groups, are increasingly common strategies to reduce maternal and newborn mortality and morbidity in LMICs [Source Card 10]. Economic evaluations suggest that most CMNC interventions are **cost-effective or highly cost-effective**, offering a promising approach for resource-constrained settings [Source Card 10].

### 5.3 Health System Strengthening
The WHO standards for quality maternal and newborn care emphasize the need for robust health systems, including:
*   **Competent and motivated human resources** [Source Card 08].
*   **Availability of essential physical resources** [Source Card 08].
*   **Robust information systems** for data collection, analysis, and utilization [Source Card 08].
*   **Effective referral systems** and communication links between different levels of care [Source Card 08].

## Key Takeaways

*   **Quality of care is a global crisis:** High maternal, newborn, and stillbirth mortality persists despite increased facility births, largely due to poor quality of care.
*   **Quality is multifaceted:** It encompasses clinical effectiveness, safety, person-centred care, and a supportive enabling environment, as outlined by WHO standards.
*   **Mistreatment is prevalent:** A significant proportion of women experience disrespect, abuse, and obstetric violence during childbirth, including physical and verbal abuse and non-consented procedures, violating their human rights.
*   **Systemic barriers are significant:** Lack of resources, inadequate staffing, poor working conditions, and low adherence to evidence-based clinical guidelines hinder quality care delivery.
*   **Multi-component strategies are effective:** Quality improvement initiatives, including facility-based interventions (training, supervision, job aids) and community-based approaches (CHW visits, women's groups), are crucial and often cost-effective.
*   **Respect and culture matter:** Respectful Maternity Care and culturally appropriate care are not optional but fundamental human rights that improve access, satisfaction, and health outcomes.

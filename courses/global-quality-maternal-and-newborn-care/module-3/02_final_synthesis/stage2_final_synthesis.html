Global Quality Maternal and Newborn Care: Bridging the Implementation Gap

Despite significant progress in increasing access to healthcare facilities for childbirth, poor quality of care remains a persistent global challenge contributing to high maternal and newborn mortality and morbidity [Source Card 12]. Globally, 2.7 million newborns die every year, 300,000 women die due to complications related to pregnancy and childbirth, and 2.6 million babies are stillborn [Source Card 12]. While effective interventions exist, there is a recognized implementation gap between knowing what works and effectively applying it on a global scale [Source Card 12]. This module explores the multifaceted nature of quality care, identifies critical gaps, and outlines strategies for improvement.

The Persistent Challenge of Suboptimal Care

Poor quality of care is a major barrier to reducing maternal and newborn mortality in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) [Source Card 09]. A systematic review highlighted significant gaps in adherence to basic, evidence-based clinical guidelines across the continuum of care [Source Card 09]. For instance, adherence to active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) was only 63.8%, and only 44.5% of women received uterotonics for postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) [Source Card 09]. Similarly, only 50.1% of women with pre-eclampsia/eclampsia received magnesium sulfate, and just 55.4% of newborns received immediate umbilical cord care [Source Card 09]. These findings underscore the urgent need for quality improvement strategies [Source Card 04, Source Card 09].

Defining Quality: Beyond Clinical Outcomes

Quality maternal and newborn care extends beyond clinical effectiveness to encompass the experience of care, human rights, and cultural appropriateness.

WHO Global Standards for Quality Care

The World Health Organization (WHO) has established eight global standards for improving the quality of maternal and newborn care in health facilities, providing a comprehensive framework for policymakers, managers, and practitioners [Source Card 08]:
1. Provision of evidence-based care: Implementing effective clinical practices during antenatal, intrapartum, and postnatal periods, including managing complications and providing care for small and sick newborns.
2. Effective communication: Ensuring clear, empathetic, and timely communication between healthcare providers, women, and their families.
3. Respectful and preserved dignity: Delivering care that upholds women's rights, treats them with dignity, and ensures privacy and confidentiality.
4. Emotional support: Providing psychosocial support to women and their families.
5. Competent and motivated human resources: Ensuring an adequate number of appropriately trained, competent, and supported healthcare professionals.
6. Availability of essential physical resources: Guaranteeing access to necessary medicines, supplies, equipment, and a safe, clean, and functional facility environment.
7. Information systems: Establishing robust data collection, analysis, and utilization systems for continuous quality improvement.
8. Referral systems: Developing and implementing effective referral pathways and communication links between different levels of care. [Source Card 08]

The Human Rights Framework: Respectful Maternity Care (RMC)

Respectful Maternity Care (RMC) is a fundamental human right for childbearing women [Source Card 06]. The White Ribbon Alliance's RMC Charter outlines seven universal rights:
1. Freedom from harm and ill-treatment.
2. The right to information, informed consent, and refusal.
3. The right to privacy and confidentiality.
4. The right to dignity and respect.
5. The right to equality, freedom from discrimination, and equitable care.
6. The right to timely healthcare and to the highest attainable standard of health.
7. The right to liberty, autonomy, and freedom from coercion. [Source Card 06]

Evidence indicates that disrespect and abuse during childbirth are widespread and undermine women's human rights [Source Card 06].

Understanding Mistreatment and Obstetric Violence

Mistreatment during facility-based childbirth is a prevalent issue. A WHO-led study across four LMICs found that more than one-third of women (41.6% of observed women) experienced at least one form of physical or verbal abuse, stigma, or discrimination [Source Card 01].
* Physical abuse (e.g., slapping, hitting, punching) was observed in approximately 14% of women [Source Card 01].
* Verbal abuse (e.g., shouting, scolding, mocking) was recorded in 38% of women [Source Card 01].
* Non-consented clinical procedures were common, with 75% of episiotomies, 59% of vaginal examinations, and 13% of caesarean births performed without consent [Source Card 01].
* Women were at highest risk of abuse during the critical period 30 minutes before birth to 15 minutes after birth [Source Card 01].
* Younger and less-educated women were identified as most vulnerable, highlighting structural inequalities [Source Card 01].

This mistreatment can be understood as obstetric violence, a form of gender-based violence experienced during childbirth within institutional medical settings [Source Card 07]. It includes physical, psychological, and emotional harm, often manifesting as a lack of informed consent, coercive medical procedures, disrespectful treatment, and the denial of a birthing person's autonomy [Source Card 07].

The Importance of Culturally Appropriate Care

The WHO strongly recommends providing culturally appropriate skilled maternity care to all women, based on moderate quality evidence [Source Card 11]. This type of care respects women's beliefs, values, and choices, and is associated with increased access to care, improved satisfaction, and better health outcomes for mothers and newborns [Source Card 11]. It requires healthcare providers to acquire cultural competencies through training and continuous professional development, and health systems to establish supportive policies and environments [Source Card 11].

Pillars of Quality Care Delivery

Achieving quality maternal and newborn care relies on a combination of competent human resources, essential physical resources, and adherence to evidence-based practices.

Competent and Supported Workforce

Midwives are central to maternal and child health, and their performance is influenced by various factors [Source Card 03].
* Positive factors include the availability of job aids (e.g., guidelines for newborn resuscitation), supportive supervision, and continuous professional development (CPD) [Source Card 03].
* Barriers to effective midwifery performance include a lack of essential resources, poor infrastructure, inadequate staffing, long working hours, low morale due to poor remuneration and limited career progression, and insufficient access to in-service training [Source Card 03].
* The WHO standards emphasize the need for an adequate number of appropriately trained, competent, and motivated healthcare professionals, supported by a conducive working environment [Source Card 08].

Essential Resources and Infrastructure

The availability of essential physical resources is critical for safe and effective care [Source Card 08]. Shortages of basic delivery kits, partographs, and life-saving drugs (e.g., oxytocin, magnesium sulfate) significantly hinder effective midwifery practice [Source Card 03]. Health facilities must guarantee access to necessary medicines, supplies, equipment, and a safe, clean, and functional environment [Source Card 08]. Robust information systems for data collection, analysis, and utilization, as well as effective referral systems with clear pathways and communication links, are also crucial for continuous quality improvement and managing complications [Source Card 08].

Evidence-Based and Appropriate Clinical Practices

Adherence to evidence-based clinical practices is fundamental for quality care [Source Card 08]. However, as noted, there are significant gaps in the implementation of key interventions [Source Card 09]. The appropriate use of interventions is also critical. For example, while caesarean sections (CS) are crucial for saving lives when medically indicated, rates above 10-15% are not associated with further reductions in maternal or newborn mortality [Source Card 05]. The focus should be on ensuring access to medically necessary CS while avoiding unnecessary procedures, emphasizing data collection and quality of care for all CS [Source Card 05].

Strategies for Quality Improvement

Improving the quality of maternal and newborn care requires comprehensive and often multi-component strategies [Source Card 04].

  • Multi-component Interventions: Most successful quality improvement (QI) initiatives involve a combination of strategies, leading to synergistic effects [Source Card 04].
  • Facility-Based QI Strategies: These include audit and feedback mechanisms, clinical training, supportive supervision, and the use of job aids and clinical guidelines to improve adherence to protocols and health worker performance [Source Card 04].
  • Community-Based Interventions: Strategies such as women's groups and the involvement of community health workers (CHWs) can improve demand for and access to maternal and newborn health services, influencing health-seeking behaviors and promoting facility births [Source Card 04]. Economic evaluations suggest that community-based maternal and newborn care (CMNC) interventions, including CHW home visits and peer support groups, are often cost-effective or highly cost-effective in LMICs, offering a promising approach for improving outcomes in resource-constrained settings [Source Card 10]. However, methodological limitations in these evaluations highlight the need for improved research methods [Source Card 10].

Key Takeaways

  • Quality of care is a global imperative: Despite increased facility births, high maternal and newborn mortality persists due to suboptimal care and an implementation gap [Source Card 12].
  • Quality is multi-dimensional: It encompasses clinical effectiveness, safety, person-centred care (including Respectful Maternity Care and freedom from obstetric violence), and cultural appropriateness [Source Card 06, Source Card 07, Source Card 08, Source Card 11].
  • Mistreatment is prevalent: A significant proportion of women experience physical abuse, verbal abuse, and non-consented procedures during childbirth, particularly younger and less-educated women [Source Card 01].
  • Systemic factors are critical: Competent and supported human resources, essential physical resources, robust information systems, and effective referral pathways are foundational to quality care [Source Card 03, Source Card 08].
  • Evidence-based practice and appropriate intervention use are essential, with significant gaps in adherence to guidelines for common complications and the need to avoid unnecessary procedures like excessive caesarean sections [Source Card 05, Source Card 09].
  • Multi-component quality improvement strategies, integrating both facility-based and community-based approaches, are most effective in improving maternal and newborn health outcomes [Source Card 04, Source Card 10].
